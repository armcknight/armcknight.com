I saw a posting about a benchmark for LLMs based on sudoku puzzles: https://pub.sakana.ai/sudoku-gpt5/

I'm not impressed by it solving a sudoku, since that's just solving a system of constraints. If you told the LLM the rules of the game, it should be able to write a piece of code that could solve any sudoku.

I'm not sure how I'd benchmark the "reasoning capabilities" of an LLM, because to me this quickly leads to the question of what is intelligence, and I don't know if we have a great way to answer that for humans. IQ tests? "Reasoning" is just a borrowed word we're applying in a new way to what an LLM is doing. Even "inference" is being extended in a similar way.

My best guess would be comprehension and interpretation. Come up with a new piece of input (text, imagery, music, etc) it hadn't seen before in its training corpus, and then ask a series of increasingly deeper questions about it, but that would also be very hard to score without any subjective bias, like tastes or preferences. And then have it iterate to produce something new, and see how you feel about it. Iterate on this process. Look at what comes out the other end, like one of my favorite party games: "telephone pictionary" aka Telestrations.

Can an LLM invent a shared secret language like you can between friends? Inside jokes, nicknames? The stuff of our social nature, not just our intellectual nature?

I've been saying for a while that AGI won't really match human levels until it can "appreciate" art, but then again, how do we even evaluate other humans' appreciate of art? We're simply tribal in our appreciation: we get along with other people that like what we like and dislike what we dislike, laugh at what we laugh at and turn our nose up at what we turn our nose up at.

In my mind, thinking can be boiled down to analytical and emotional. Analytical thinking, our intellectual nature, is already solved; anything that can be transformed into mathematical constraints can be worked by a computer.

Emotions are also systems, albeit complex biochemical ones involving brain structures, neurotransmitters, hormones, where balances evolve over times and are modulated by continuous sensory input. Another thing I've been saying for a while is that the advent of AGI may not be production of a new thing external to ourselves with a new capability, but simply the realization that we aren't as complicated or special as we think. Of course we'll probably call it something else besides AGI by that time, similar to how we've gone from global warming to climate change, from the big crunch to the big rip to the heat death of the universe.

If we "solve" emotions, we'll be no different than a sudoku puzzle, maybe an incomplete one with more than one solution, but nonetheless, one in which all possible paths can be laid out before us, each able to be evaluated in its turn. Watson solved chess, AlphaGo solved go.

Can statistical computation solve destiny?

The only thing that's left for us to solve at that point is that of choice. How do we predict which of those paths a given human would take? Is it based on their intelligence? Their emotional state at that moment?

Choice is a cliché both ancient and modern, from the free will of Adam and Eve in the Bible, to Neo and Trinity in The Matrix. What is choice? Is it the probabilistic component that randomizes our reality and keeps it nondeterministically rich? Can it, too, be backed out to the seed that germinated our pseudorandom processes, by inspecting our initial conditions and prior stimuli? Photonic mutation of a neuron in an individual's hippocampus at just the right time? That, too, could be modeled, given sufficient resources.

And maybe all the ideas underlying what I'm saying through these words, graphemes, morphemes can be modeled too. None of those implicitly hold the ideas themselves. They're just the imperfect discretizations of my ideas, they're all I have to work with based on how my mind was trained. Same as LLMs.

One last thing I've long said, from before I ever had heard of AI: religion is the tool by which we explain the unexplainable. The frontier of what we can explain has continually expanded, but there seems to always be more before us, like the Ba'Há'í model of progressive revelation. We still can't explain free will, not that I've seen anyways, so we will interpret it through lenses of religion or science, depending on the knowledge and proclivities of the person you ask.

And it seems like we can always ask another why. Why are we able to see art? Because the photons reflecting off the canvas, with various wavelengths being absorbed to varying degrees at different locations, enter our eye, are focused onto our retina, which stimulate different neuron structures in our brains, causing series of waves of activity through networks of neurons, mediated by neurotransmitters and hormones. But why? Why are those structures and chemicals there? Well, it goes all the way back to creation by God, or the big bang. But why does God exist, or why did the big bang happen? Why did the elements that came to be, come to be?

The answer to that, as far as I can tell, is that it simply isn't, didn't. Not the way we're talking about it, or understand it. Our discretization of reality into "elements" is only a crude approximation, and the true reality is that all of existence is a more integral, continuous spectrum of matter and energy. Whatever integral or continuous or spectrum are supposed to represent.

But what is matter and energy, why do they exist, why do we disambiguate them? Why is the universe made of matter and energy, or the underlying phenomenon we try to explain using that approximating discretization?

Once i reach the frontier of the questions I can even ask, it sure is easy to just shrug my shoulders and say: God.

What would AI say? It would only be a recombination of all of what humans could have possibly asked or explained, in their imperfect knowledge, before the cutoff of its training date.

And that actually ossifies the the frontier of our understanding. It can't invent new understanding. Unless like that photon, it knocks something loose in one of our own minds. But, to me, that's not qualitatively different from a human reading another human's writing and experiencing the same Eureka! moment. It's all just words. LLMs have already passed the Turing test, we don't even know who or what is writing some of the things we read on the Internet.

And for that matter, LLMs can't "feel" what I "feel" when I look at a Van Gogh painting, at least not until we can build an exact replica of a human body, with all the intricacy previously described. After all, a human body is a system, a system of systems, which can be modeled and replicated, given sufficient resources.

At that point, if we reach it, we will know we aren't special, but rather are simply an arrangement of particles that can express theretofore unexplainable emergent phenomena such as emotion.

Voila, AGI! Our own intelligence, general instead of special, an artifice of true intelligence, of which there's no such thing the dichotomy between the two having been rendered [mu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_(negative)). You know, like a cow's opinion.
